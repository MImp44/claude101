Request for
Approval

Come with a hypothesis, not just a request.

Access to Claude at Disney is approved through the Disney Developer portal, but please connect with your Leader first. The quality of your request matters. Take five minutes to define a clear hypothesis and use case before submitting. It strengthens your request, speeds up approval, and makes sure you're set up to get real value out of the tool once you're in.

What a Good Approval Request Looks Like

01
Singular
Focused on one specific opportunity, not a broad wish list. Pick one workflow, one deliverable, one recurring problem you want to improve.
02
Clear
Written in plain language. No jargon required to understand it. If you can't explain it in two sentences, it's not clear enough yet.
03
Testable
Framed so you'll be able to tell whether it worked. Time saved, output quality, fewer revision rounds. Pick something you can actually observe.

Write Your Hypothesis

The Hypothesis Format

Use this structure to write your hypothesis before submitting for approval. Keep it to one or two sentences. The goal is to be specific enough that your approver can picture exactly what you're trying to do.

Hypothesis Template

"There may be an opportunity to improve how [I / my team] does [specific task or workflow] during [when this happens] so that [expected outcome]." Example: "There may be an opportunity to improve how the Digital team drafts social content calendars during title launch windows so that we spend less time on first drafts and more time on strategy and review."

Three Questions to Answer Before You Submit

Before submitting your access request, make sure you can answer all three. These are the questions your approver will have, so answering them upfront makes the process faster for everyone.

Approval Checklist

1. What task are you trying to improve? Be specific. Not "use AI for marketing" — pick one workflow, one deliverable, one recurring problem. 2. How will you measure whether it worked? Time saved, output quality, fewer revision rounds, faster turnaround. Pick something you can actually observe. 3. Who else on your team will benefit? If the use case applies to more than one person, say so. It strengthens the case for access.

Use Case Template — Copy, Fill In, Submit

Copy this template, fill it in, and include it with your access request at developer.disney.com. The more specific your answers, the faster the approval and the better your outcome once you're set up.

Use Case Template

Role: [Your title and function] Hypothesis: There may be an opportunity to improve how [I/my team] does [specific task] during [workflow/timing] so that [expected outcome]. Use case: [One paragraph describing the specific task, current process, and what Claude would do differently] Success looks like: [What would tell you it's working — time, quality, volume, or output] Estimated usage: [Daily / weekly / monthly — light / moderate / heavy]

Make It Specific

The clearest use cases get approved fastest and deliver the most value. "I want to use AI" won't get approved quickly and won't set you up for success when it does. "I want to use Claude to draft press release first drafts and subject line variants for our title campaigns, which currently takes 2-3 hours per project" will. Specificity is the ask.

Identifying
Use Cases

Find where Claude fits into your actual work.

The function chapters in this guide give you a menu of proven use cases. But the best use cases are the ones you find yourself: the tasks you do every week that Claude could handle in minutes. This chapter gives you the frameworks and prompts to uncover them.

The Use Case Opportunity Framework

01
Repetitive Writing
Any writing task you do in the same format more than twice a month. Email templates, weekly reports, social captions, briefing summaries, Claude can draft these in seconds once it understands your format and voice.
02
Research & Synthesis
Any task where you need to read multiple inputs and distill them into something smaller. Research reports, competitive reviews, stakeholder summaries, coverage roundups, Claude reads fast and summarizes well.
03
First Drafts
Anything you currently start from a blank page. Briefs, pitches, proposals, presentations, press releases, starting from Claude's draft and editing is almost always faster than starting from nothing.
04
Format Conversion
Turning one format into another. Bullet points into prose. Data into narrative. A long report into a one-pager. A transcript into action items. If the content exists and just needs reshaping, Claude handles this well.
05
Review & Pressure-Testing
Getting a second opinion before something goes out. Copy review, strategy critique, brief evaluation, positioning review. Claude will find the weaknesses you've stopped seeing because you've been too close to the work.
06
Data Interpretation
Turning numbers into language. Performance reports, campaign results, research findings, budget variance, Claude reads the data and tells you the story, so you can focus on what to do about it.

Use Case Discovery, Ask Claude to Find Your Opportunities

The Work Audit Prompt

Run this once and you'll have a personalized Claude use case map for your role. Describe your actual week to Claude and ask it to identify every place it could save you time. The more specific your description, the more actionable the output.

Example Prompt, Beginner

"I work in [your function] at Disney Marketing. Here is what a typical week looks like for me: [list your 6-8 most common tasks, be specific]. For each task, tell me: (1) whether Claude could help, (2) what that help would look like in practice, and (3) a specific example prompt I could try today. Prioritize by time savings."

The Bottleneck Prompt

For when you know something is slow but you're not sure if Claude can help. Describe the bottleneck and ask Claude to diagnose whether it's the kind of problem AI can actually solve and what the best approach would be.

Example Prompt, Intermediate

"Here is a workflow that takes me too long: [describe it step by step, what you start with, what you do, what you end up with]. Is this something Claude can improve? If so: which steps can Claude handle, which need human judgment, and what would the new workflow look like? Show me what the first step would look like in practice."

The Document You Dread Prompt

Most marketers have one recurring document they hate writing, the monthly report, the campaign debrief, the exec summary, the budget commentary. Claude can take that document off your plate. Start here.

Example Prompt, Beginner

"There is a document I have to write regularly that I find tedious: [describe what it is, what goes into it, who reads it, and what format it usually takes]. Here is an example of a recent version: [paste or describe]. I want you to help me build a reusable template and process so this takes me 10 minutes instead of an hour. Start by asking me any questions you need."

The Meeting Prep Prompt

Before any high-stakes meeting, a leadership review, an agency briefing, a cross-functional alignment session, Claude can help you prepare sharper talking points, anticipate objections, and walk in more confident. This is one of the most underused daily use cases.

Example Prompt, Beginner

"I have a [meeting type] in [timeframe] with [who will be in the room]. The goal is [what you want to accomplish]. Here is the relevant context: [paste any briefs, data, or background]. Help me: (1) identify the 3 things I most need to be ready to answer, (2) draft my opening framing in 2-3 sentences, and (3) flag any risks or gaps in my position I should address before walking in."

The "Is This a Good Claude Task?" Test

Not everything is a good use of Claude. Use this quick mental checklist and this prompt, to quickly evaluate whether a task is worth delegating to Claude before investing time in prompting it.

Quick Evaluation Checklist

Good Claude tasks typically have:, A clear output format (email, summary, options list, calendar), Available input (a brief, data, background context you can share), A "good enough" bar (where 80% there is still useful), Repeatable structure (you've done this type of task before) Be cautious when:, The task requires confidential data you shouldn't share, The output needs to be precisely accurate (financial figures, legal language), The judgment required is deeply relationship-dependent, You can't evaluate whether Claude's output is correct

The Best Use Case Discovery Method

Spend one week writing down every task that takes you more than 20 minutes. At the end of the week, paste that list into Claude and ask: "Which of these could I do faster with AI, and how?" You will find 3-5 opportunities you hadn't thought of. The marketers who get the most value from Claude aren't the ones who know the most about AI, they're the ones who are most honest about where their time goes.

Responsible Use

Use Claude confidently and carefully.

Claude is a powerful tool, and Disney has access to it across the company. That means being thoughtful about how you use it. This chapter covers what to share, what to protect, how Disney's deployment works, and how to use Claude in a way that's effective and compliant.

What to Share vs. What to Protect

Safe to Share with Claude

These types of content are appropriate to work with in Claude for standard marketing tasks. When in doubt, ask yourself: would I share this in a work email to a vendor or agency partner?

Generally appropriate

— Approved campaign briefs and creative directions, Published brand guidelines and messaging frameworks, Publicly available research and market data, General audience and campaign strategy (non-confidential), Anonymized or aggregated performance data, Draft copy and marketing materials for review, Meeting notes and action items (non-sensitive), Job descriptions, templates, and process documents

Handle with Care, Do Not Upload Without Authorization

These categories require explicit authorization or should be avoided entirely. When uncertain, consult your manager, Legal, or the Anthropic @ TWDC team before uploading.

Use caution or avoid

— Unreleased film details, titles, or story elements under NDA, Talent contracts, deal terms, or personal talent information, Individual employee performance data or HR records, Detailed financial projections, budgets with sensitive line items, Legal documents, settlements, or privileged communications, Personal information about individuals (employees or consumers), Third-party confidential information shared under NDA, Security credentials, passwords, or API keys

How Disney's Claude Deployment Works

Organization-Level Sharing, Know the Risk

Disney operates a single shared Organization in Claude. This has an important implication: anything you share at the organization level, Projects, prompts, or documents, is visible to all Claude users across Disney. This is easy to overlook when you're moving quickly.

How to manage sharing correctly

Default setting for new Projects: Private Share with specific people: Project Settings > Share > add individuals Share with your team: add team members by Disney email Share org-wide: only do this intentionally, for content meant for all Disney Claude users Rule of thumb: Start private. Share selectively when the content is ready and appropriate for a broader audience.

Usage Is Tracked and Billed to Your Segment

Claude usage at Disney is tracked at both the segment and individual level. Your usage is billed to your segment's tech budget, not to you personally. Understanding your usage helps you stay within your tier and helps your segment manage costs responsibly.

How to check your usage

Open the Claude desktop app Click Settings (bottom-left corner) Select "Usage" You'll see: tokens used, your tier limit, and your reset date Note: Tiers are upper limits, you're only billed for actual usage. To adjust your tier: return to developer.disney.com and modify your access request.

Copyright, Brand Safety & Accuracy

Copyright & Originality

Claude generates original text based on patterns in training data. It does not copy from sources verbatim, but output is not automatically copyright-cleared for all uses. Apply the same judgment you would to any external vendor's work: review, edit, and take ownership before publishing.

Good practice

— Treat Claude output as a first draft, not final copy, Do not ask Claude to reproduce song lyrics, book passages, or screenplay text verbatim, For anything going to print, broadcast, or paid media: have it reviewed like any other copy, Do not use Claude to generate images or copy that impersonates real people or existing IP, When using Claude for competitive research: summarize insights, don't reproduce competitor content

Accuracy & Hallucination

Claude can generate confident-sounding text that is factually incorrect, a behavior called "hallucination." This is more likely when Claude is asked about specific facts, statistics, dates, or sourced claims. Always verify before using any Claude-generated factual claim in an external-facing document.

How to reduce hallucination risk

High risk tasks (always verify): specific statistics, market data, quotes, dates, legal claims, financial figures Low risk tasks: tone review, copy drafts, structure suggestions, formatting, brainstorming Prompt technique: Add "If you are uncertain about any specific fact in your response, flag it explicitly" to any prompt that requires factual accuracy. For research tasks: ask Claude to cite where it's drawing from, then verify independently.

When to Escalate

If you're ever unsure whether something is appropriate to share with Claude, just ask first. Post in the Get Help channel on the Anthropic @ TWDC Team, or email ENTERPRISE.TECH.AI.DEVELOPER.TOOLS.SUPPORT@disney.com. It is always better to ask than to have to undo something after the fact.

Setup Guide

User Set-Up

Your first 30 minutes with Claude, done right.

Most people open Claude, type something, and call it good. That works fine. But you're leaving a lot on the table. This section walks through the setup steps and habits that make a real difference. Takes about 30 minutes once, pays off every day after.

Step-by-Step Setup

Step 1, Access Claude Through Disney's Enterprise License

Disney employees access Claude through our enterprise agreement. You don't need a personal account or separate billing. Request access through the Disney Developer portal and use your Disney SSO to log in. Set your usage limit to match your tier — your access request will show your cap. As a starting point: Users around $25/month, Champions around $100/month, Leaders per your full-access tier. You can adjust after the first few weeks based on actual usage.

What's included under your enterprise seat

Access to all Claude models including Opus and Sonnet / Claude.ai chat (web + mobile) / Cowork desktop app / Claude Code (CLI) / Projects & persistent memory / Skills and Connectors / Disney SSO, no separate Anthropic account needed

Step 2, Don't Make the Custom Instructions Mistake

The mistake I see most often: people write personality instructions instead of practical ones. "You are helpful and warm and creative", Claude already knows that. What it doesn't know is who you are, what you're working on, and how you want things formatted. Write for the gaps, not the baseline.

Strong custom instructions, what to include

Who you are: "I'm a [your role — e.g. campaign manager, publicist, analyst] on the [team] team in Disney Marketing. I work on [what you actually do day to day]." What you typically need: "I usually need: campaign strategy, copy drafts, research synthesis, exec presentations." How to format outputs: "Default to bullet points for analysis, prose for copy, tables for comparisons." Key constraints: "Always give me 2-3 options. Flag risks. Keep executive-facing content concise." What to skip: "No preamble. No 'Great question!' openers. Get to the answer."

Step 3, Learn the ICC Prompting Framework

ICC is the simplest way to consistently get better outputs: Identity (who Claude should be), Context (what you're working on), Criteria (what good looks like). Most people hit one or two of these. All three together is the difference between a generic answer and something you can actually use. Try it a few times and it becomes second nature.

ICC Framework, Template

IDENTITY: "You are a senior Disney marketing strategist..." CONTEXT: "I'm working on the awareness campaign for [title], targeting families, 6 weeks from release. Here is the brief: [paste]." CRITERIA: "Write 3 tagline options. Max 8 words each. Tone: warm and confident, not hype. Bold the one you'd recommend and explain why."

Step 4, Use the Context Interview (Pro Tip)

Instead of spending 20 minutes crafting the perfect prompt, flip it. Tell Claude what you're trying to do, then ask it to interview you, 3 to 5 questions before it starts writing anything. It'll pull out exactly what it needs, including things you probably forgot to mention. Genuinely one of the most effective moves in here, and almost nobody uses it.

The Context Interview, Prompt

"Before you start, I want to make sure you have everything you need. I'm going to [describe the task]. Before writing anything, ask me 3–5 targeted questions that would most improve the quality of your output. Once I've answered, then proceed."

Step 5, Set Up a Project (The Right Way)

Projects let Claude remember everything about a workstream so you never have to re-explain it. Set one up per title campaign or function area. The instructions are what matter most, be specific about the campaign, the audience, the tone you want. Think of it as the brief Claude reads at the start of every conversation in that project.

Project Instructions, Disney Marketing Template

"You are a senior marketing strategist embedded on [Title], a Disney [animated/live-action/series] targeting [audience]. We are currently in [campaign phase]. Key messages: [X, Y, Z]. Brand voice: [describe]. My role: [your title/function]. Default outputs to [format]. Always offer 2–3 options rather than a single answer. Flag risks. Be direct, I don't need warmth or encouragement, I need good work fast."

Step 6, Connect Your Tools with Connectors

Connectors let Claude reach into your actual tools, Drive, Slack, Asana, Notion, and more. Once connected, you stop copy-pasting. Claude reads your briefs directly, checks Slack, pulls from Notion. Takes a few minutes to set up and meaningfully changes how you use it day to day.

Connectors to set up first

Priority 1: Google Drive (campaign briefs, research docs, brand guidelines) Priority 2: Slack (team updates, campaign status, approvals) Priority 3: Asana or your PM tool (deadlines, owners, task status) Priority 4: Notion or SharePoint (knowledge bases, templates, references) Setup: Settings > Integrations > Connect each tool with your work account

Step 7, Know Which Model to Use When

There are three Claude models and they're not interchangeable. Sonnet is your everyday workhorse, fast and strong for most tasks. Opus is for complex strategy work, synthesis jobs, or anything where you want the ceiling raised. Haiku is quick and lightweight, good for simple tasks. Default to Sonnet, reach for Opus when the work really matters.

Model Selection Guide

Claude Sonnet, Use for: daily copy drafting, email, social content, research summaries, quick analysis. Fast, highly capable, lowest usage cost. This is your default. Claude Opus, Use for: complex strategy documents, multi-source synthesis, creative concepting, nuanced positioning reviews, anything where depth matters more than speed. Claude Haiku, Use for: quick factual lookups, simple reformatting, short tasks where speed is the only requirement. Rarely needed for marketing work.

Step 8, Use the Desktop App (Cowork) for File-Heavy Work

Browser chat is great for quick back-and-forth. But if you work with documents regularly, decks, reports, spreadsheets, download the Cowork desktop app. Drop a file in, have Claude work through it, and get a polished deliverable back as a .docx, .pptx, or .xlsx. Once you try it for real work, you won't want to go back to just the browser.

When to use Desktop App vs. Browser

Use Browser when: quick prompts, research questions, back-and-forth drafting, anything that doesn't involve files. Use Desktop App (Cowork) when: working with uploaded documents, need polished file outputs (Word, PowerPoint, Excel), running multi-step tasks against your own materials, want outputs saved to a project folder you can access directly.

The Most Powerful Combination in Claude

Projects + Skills together is the combination that changes everything. A Project stores your context, campaign, audience, brand voice. A Skill runs a structured workflow for a specific task. Use a Skill inside a Project and you get both: a workflow that already knows your work. You stop explaining from scratch. You stop getting generic outputs. It starts feeling less like a tool and more like someone who's always up to speed.

Best Practices

Getting the
Best Out of Claude.

Five modes. One principle: specificity wins.

Claude has a few different modes and each one works differently. Chat, Cowork, Skills, Projects, Code. Here's what each is actually good for.

Chat is where most people start and where most people stop pushing. These are the five habits that separate a decent result from one you can actually use.

01
Set the role and context first
Tell Claude who it is and what you're working on before giving the task. "You are a Disney marketing strategist working on a live-action title campaign for families..." This anchors every response that follows.
02
Front-load your constraints
State format, length, audience, and tone before asking. "Give me 5 options in a table. Max 12 words each. Tone: warm, not hype." Constraints aren't limitations, they're instructions for quality.
03
Iterate, don't re-ask
When output misses, tell Claude exactly what to change instead of starting over. "Keep the structure. Sharpen the first sentence. Cut the second paragraph." This is faster and produces better results.
04
Ask Claude to critique its own work
After receiving output, ask: "What's the weakest part of what you just wrote, and how would you fix it?" Claude's editorial instinct is genuinely useful and underused.
05
Ask for options, not just answers
When you're not sure what you want, ask for range. "Give me three versions: one emotional, one informational, one punchy." Options let you decide, rather than argue with one response.
Prompt Comparison, Before & After
Weak prompt

"Write a tagline for our new movie."

vs.
Strong prompt

Cowork is the desktop app. It's where Claude actually works with your files, not just talks about them. Drop in a deck, a brief, a spreadsheet, and get a real deliverable back. Think of it less like a chat and more like a working session with someone sitting next to you.

01
Bring your source materials
Upload your brief, your data, your existing deck before asking. Claude works from your actual materials, not abstractions. The more context you load, the more accurate the output.
02
Think in deliverables, not questions
Instead of "What should our social strategy be?" say "Build me a 4-week social content calendar for [title]. I'm uploading the campaign brief now." Cowork is built for task completion, not Q&A.
03
Ask for polished file outputs
Cowork can produce .docx, .pptx, and .xlsx files. Ask for the format you'll actually use: "Build this as a Word document formatted for executive review" or "Turn this into a slide deck."
04
Use multi-step task sequencing
Break complex tasks explicitly: "Step 1: Read this brief and identify the 3 biggest strategic gaps. Step 2: Rewrite the brief to address them. Step 3: Write a one-page executive summary."
05
Save work to your project folder
Cowork saves files to a persistent workspace folder on your machine. Use it as a shared scratchpad, Claude can read and build on previous outputs across sessions.

Skills are pre-built workflows. Instead of building a prompt from scratch for something like a campaign plan or a brand audit, you call a Skill and Claude runs the whole structured process for you. The Marketing plugin comes loaded with these:

Campaign Plan
Full brief with objectives, channels, calendar, metrics
Content Creation
Draft for any channel, format, or audience
Brand Review
Audit copy against brand voice and messaging
Email Sequence
Multi-email drip with logic, timing, copy
Competitive Brief
Positioning comparison, gaps, opportunities
Performance Report
Metrics narrative with recommendations
Load context before invoking a Skill
Tell Claude what you're working on before triggering a Skill. Better input produces better structured output. A Skill is a format, the quality still depends on the context you provide.
Chain Skills together
Use Content Creation to draft copy, then immediately run Brand Review on the output. Two Skills in sequence saves significant review time.
Skills vs. regular chat
Use a Skill when you want a structured, full deliverable (a full campaign brief, a full email sequence). Use chat for quick questions, iteration, and creative exploration.

Projects are how you stop explaining yourself over and over. Set one up for a workstream, write your instructions once, and every conversation that follows inherits the context, campaign, audience, tone. Claude knows it going in.

01
Create a Project for every title campaign
Name it "[Title], Marketing" and populate it with the campaign brief, positioning doc, and key messages. Every conversation in that Project starts with full context.
02
Write strong Project Instructions
This is the most important step. Tell Claude: its role, what the campaign is, the brand voice, target audience, key constraints, and what outputs you typically need.
03
Use Projects for recurring workstreams
Not just title campaigns. "Social Strategy 2026," "Publicity Templates," "Research Synthesis", any ongoing work benefits from a persistent context.
04
Update instructions as the campaign evolves
As your campaign moves from awareness to release to post-launch, update the Project Instructions to reflect the current phase, priorities, and any shifted messaging.
Sample Project Instructions, Title Campaign

"You are a senior marketing strategist for [Title], a Disney animated feature targeting families with children 4–10. The campaign is in the awareness phase (12 weeks out from release). Key messages: [X, Y, Z]. Brand voice: warm, adventurous, hopeful, never ironic or edgy. Always provide 2–3 options rather than a single answer. Format outputs for senior marketing leadership unless I specify otherwise. When I ask for copy, default to Disney brand tone guidelines."

You don't need to know how to code. Claude writes it. You just tell it what you need. For our team, this is most useful for data work, quick automations, and building shareable reports without pulling in a developer.

01
Analyze spreadsheet data with a prompt
Upload a CSV or Excel file and ask: "Summarize the trends in this campaign performance data. What's working, what's underperforming, and what would you optimize first?"
02
Build shareable HTML dashboards
Ask Claude to turn data into a visual report: "Convert this media plan data into a clean one-page HTML summary I can share with my team via email." No dev resources needed.
03
Get Excel/Sheets formulas on demand
Describe what you need in plain English: "Write an Excel formula that calculates cost-per-thousand-impressions across these five media line items and flags any over $25 CPM."
04
Automate repetitive data formatting
"I have 300 rows of press coverage in this CSV. Reformat it: sort by publication tier, add a 'reach estimate' column, remove duplicates, and output a clean Excel file."
05
Start simple, complexity scales
Start with what you need right now. If Claude's output isn't quite right, describe what to fix in plain language. You're directing, not programming.
Function 01

Creative

Your thinking partner and first-draft engine.

For Creative, Claude is most useful before production gets involved: concept exploration, copy generation, brief writing, brand voice checks. It won't replace your creative judgment. What it does is dramatically reduce the blank-page time and give your team more options to react to.

Claude Use Cases, Creative

Campaign Concept Exploration

When you're walking into a concepting session with nothing on the wall. Give Claude the brief and you'll have 8–10 territory options in a few minutes. Your team reacts to something instead of starting from zero.

Example Prompt

"You are a Disney marketing creative director. Here is the campaign brief: [paste brief]. Generate 8 distinct concept territories for this campaign. For each, give: a one-line concept name, the emotional truth it's built on, and a sample headline. Vary the emotional register across options, include at least one surprising or unexpected direction."

️

Copy Generation, Headlines, Taglines & Social

When you need volume quickly, taglines, headlines, social copy. Claude generates 10–20 options across different tones so you're curating, not writing from scratch.

Example Prompt

"Write 12 tagline options for [title/campaign]. Audience: [describe]. Tone: [describe, e.g., 'warm and adventurous, not sentimental']. Constraint: max 8 words. Organize them into three groups: emotional, action-oriented, and unexpected. Bold the two you think are strongest and say why."

Brand Voice Review

Before anything ships, especially if it was written fast, written by a vendor, or written by someone newer to Disney's voice. Claude catches what's off-brand before it becomes a problem.

Example Prompt

"Act as a Disney brand guardian. Review the following copy against Disney's brand voice: warm, confident, story-first, never ironic or snarky. Flag any lines that feel off-tone, explain why, and suggest an alternative for each. Here is the copy: [paste copy]."

Creative Rationale Writing

When you've made a creative call and need to write the 'why' behind it for leadership or partners. Give Claude the direction and a clear rationale is ready in minutes. You refine, you don't write from scratch.

Example Prompt

"Write a 250-word creative rationale for the following campaign direction: [describe the concept, the visual approach, and the emotional hook]. The audience is [describe]. Write it for a senior leadership review, confident and concise, not pitchy. Explain: what insight it's built on, why this approach serves the audience, and what makes it distinctly Disney."

Production Asset Briefs

When you're briefing a photography team, an edit house, or an external partner. Claude takes the concept and writes a clear production brief, your partners know exactly what they're delivering without three rounds of back-and-forth.

Example Prompt

"Write a production brief for a social media photography shoot for [title/campaign]. Campaign concept: [describe]. Required deliverables: [list formats and quantities]. Tone and visual direction: [describe]. Include sections for: objective, visual references, required shots, technical specs, and restrictions. Write it for an external photography vendor."

Creative Pro Tip

Ask Claude to break its own rules. After generating tagline or concept options, add: "Now give me one that deliberately breaks from Disney convention, unexpected, but could still work." That contrarian option is usually what sparks the best conversation in the room.

Function 04

Strategy

From insight to brief, faster and sharper.

Strategy is synthesis: taking research, market context, and audience understanding and turning it into the brief everyone else works from. Claude is fast at synthesis and good at first structure. That means you spend more time challenging what's in the brief instead of writing it.

Claude Use Cases, Strategy

Campaign Strategy Development

When you have audience data, competitive context, and a business goal sitting in separate documents and need them synthesized into a recommendation. Claude handles the assembly work.

Example Prompt

"You are a senior Disney marketing strategist. Here are the key inputs for the [title] campaign: audience research summary [paste], business objective [state], competitive context [paste or describe], and release timing [state]. Write a one-page campaign strategy recommendation covering: the audience insight this is built on, our positioning, the campaign idea territory, and channel priorities. Format for a Creative briefing."

Creative Brief Writing

Writing a brief that inspires without over-prescribing is genuinely hard. Claude drafts the structure from your inputs and then helps you pressure-test whether the proposition is actually single-minded, or just trying to do too much.

Example Prompt

"Write a creative brief for [title/campaign]. Audience: [describe]. Business objective: [state]. Key insight: [state]. Single-minded proposition: [state]. Tone: [describe]. Mandatories: [list]. Use standard brief format with these sections: Background, Audience, Objective, Insight, Proposition, Tone, Mandatories. After writing the brief, tell me: is this proposition truly single-minded, or is it trying to do too much?"

Audience Insight Synthesis

When you have research in hand and need the so-what, not just the summary. Claude tells you what the data means for the campaign, not just what it says.

Example Prompt

"Here is a summary of our audience research for [title]: [paste]. Don't just summarize the data, tell me: what's the single most important insight for our campaign strategy? What does this audience actually want to feel? And what does this research suggest we should NOT do in our marketing? Write it as a 1-page insight brief for a strategic briefing."

Positioning Review & Pressure-Test

Before you lock a positioning statement, have Claude push on it. It'll tell you if someone else could say the same thing, if it's too generic, and what would make it sharper. Its willingness to find weaknesses is more useful than getting a nod from someone who isn't really pushing back.

Example Prompt

"Here is our proposed positioning statement for [title/campaign]: [paste]. Play devil's advocate: (1) Does this meaningfully differentiate us from competitors? Name 2 competitors who could say the same thing. (2) Is there a risk it's too generic? (3) What would make it sharper or more ownable? Be direct, I need honest critique, not validation."

Go-to-Market Planning

When you need to build the phased plan, what's happening when, in what sequence, across which channels. Claude drafts the GTM framework from your inputs so you're organizing against a structure, not filling in a blank doc.

Example Prompt

"Build a go-to-market plan framework for [title]. Release date: [date]. Current date: [date]. Campaign phases: awareness, consideration, release, post-release. For each phase, recommend: timing window, the primary campaign objective, key marketing activities (paid, owned, earned), and the leading metric. Format as a table. Flag any timing risks I should be aware of."

Strategy Pro Tip

The most underused prompt in Strategy: "What are we assuming that might be wrong?" Ask this after any strategy document Claude produces. It surfaces the hidden assumptions baked into your work, the ones that could sink the campaign if they're off. Treat it as a pre-mortem before anything gets locked.

Example Prompt

Keep the brand and emotional territory consistent with the global campaign, but adjust: (1) the key message hierarchy to reflect what resonates locally, (2) any references that won't land, (3) the tone if culturally appropriate. Global/Local Adaptation Guidelines

When you need to publish guidance to local markets on what they can and can't touch. Claude writes it in clear, actionable language, specific enough to protect consistency without reading like a legal document.

Example Prompt

"Write localization guidelines for the [title] global campaign for distribution to local marketing teams. Market Learnings Synthesis

Post-campaign, when market reports are coming in from everywhere and you need to pull them into something coherent. Claude reads across all of them and finds the patterns, what worked universally, what was market-specific, and what to carry forward.

Example Prompt

"Here are post-campaign summaries from [X] markets for [title]: [paste or describe each]. Synthesize these into a global learnings document. Identify: (1) what worked across markets (top 3 universal insights), (2) what worked in specific markets only (and why it might be market-specific), (3) what underperformed globally, and (4) 3 recommendations for the next global campaign. Format for a global marketing leadership review."

International Pro Tip

Always treat Claude's cultural review as a starting point, not a final answer. Claude has broad cultural knowledge but not the lived experience of a local market expert. Use it to identify the right questions to bring to your local teams, not to replace them. Prompt it to tell you where it's uncertain.

Function 08

Finance

Budget clarity. Spend confidence. ROI you can explain.

Every marketer ends up doing finance work: budgets, spend reporting, ROI conversations, vendor reviews. Claude won't replace your Finance partner, but it'll help you walk into those conversations better prepared and with better materials. The goal is clarity instead of uncertainty going in.

Claude Use Cases, Finance for Marketers

Budget Allocation Framework

When you have a total budget and need to work through how to allocate it across channels, activities, and phases. Claude builds the framework and flags the tradeoffs, so you're making informed decisions, not educated guesses.

Example Prompt

"I have a $[X]M marketing budget for [title]. Campaign objective: [state]. Audience: [describe]. Channels we're considering: [list]. Build a budget allocation framework across: creative production, paid media (by channel), PR/events, digital/owned, and contingency. For each category, give a recommended percentage range and the rationale. Then flag: where does this allocation carry the most risk, and where is there room to flex if we need to cut?"

Budget Variance Commentary

For monthly and quarterly finance reviews. Claude writes variance commentary that explains the business reason behind each line item, not just restates what the numbers say. It's the difference between useful commentary and labeled data.

Example Prompt

"Here is our actual vs. plan marketing spend for [period]: [paste table or key line items with variances]. Write the budget variance commentary for our monthly marketing finance review. For each significant variance (over ±10%), explain: what drove it, whether it was planned or unplanned, and the business impact. Format as brief bullet points per line item, with a 2-sentence overall summary at the top."

ROI Calculation & Explanation

When you need to calculate, frame, or present marketing ROI and you know the follow-up questions are coming. Claude builds the logic and writes the narrative so you can walk through it confidently, including flagging what you can and can't definitively prove.

Example Prompt

"Help me build an ROI framework for the [title] marketing campaign. Inputs I have: total marketing spend [$X], estimated incremental box office attributable to marketing [methodology/estimate], and any brand equity metrics if relevant. Walk me through the calculation, flag any assumptions I'm making, and write a 150-word ROI narrative I could use in a leadership presentation. Keep it honest, flag what we can and can't prove."

Cost Modeling & Scenario Planning

When you're stress-testing spend scenarios before a Finance conversation, "what does a 15% cut look like?" or "what does an extra $500K get us?" Claude builds the logic and surfaces the tradeoffs so you're not going in blind.

Example Prompt

"I need to model three budget scenarios for [title]: (1) current plan at $[X], (2) a 20% reduction, and (3) a 15% increase. For each scenario, describe: which activities get cut or added, the expected impact on campaign reach and key metrics, and the strategic risk. I'll use this to frame a budget conversation with Finance. Format as a comparison table with a brief narrative summary for each scenario."

Vendor Proposal Review

Before you sign off on a significant vendor or agency proposal. Claude reviews the scope, flags anything that looks off relative to the work, and surfaces negotiation opportunities. A fast second set of eyes before money moves.

Example Prompt

"Review this vendor proposal for $[X]: [paste proposal or key line items]. Flag: (1) any line items that seem high relative to the scope of work, (2) anything that's unclear or ambiguous in what's being delivered, (3) any standard items that appear to be missing. Then suggest 3 specific negotiation points I should raise before signing. Context: [describe what the vendor is doing and the project scope]."

Finance Pro Tip

Prepare for Finance conversations, don't just walk into them. Before any budget discussion, prompt: "I'm presenting a budget request for [X]. What questions will a Finance partner likely ask and what data or logic do I need to answer them?" That turns a reactive conversation into a prepared one.

For each reporter, give me: (1) their main story or theme this week, (2) the narrative angle they're taking on Disney or the broader industry, and (3) anything to watch — questions they might bring our way or storylines building in their coverage. Then close with a 3-4 sentence overall read: what are these reporters collectively focused on right now, and what does that mean for our comms posture this week? Here are the stories and posts: [paste headlines, links, excerpts, or social posts by reporter]"

Executive Message Development

Corporate Narrative Framing

When Disney needs to talk about a complex topic — a strategic shift, an industry trend, a business decision — the framing has to be precise. Rapid Response Options

When something breaks and you need holding statement options fast — before you've had time to fully assess. Claude generates a range of response postures so your team has options to evaluate rather than starting from blank. Always review and apply judgment before anything goes out.

Function 10

Office of
the CMO

Keep the CMO prepared, informed, and ahead of the week.

This section is built for the senior assistants and managers who support the CMO day to day. The job is operational and high-stakes: the right information gets to the right person at the right moment. Claude helps you build the briefs, prep the meetings, synthesize the inputs, and draft the communications that keep the office running at its best.

Claude Use Cases — Office of the CMO

Daily Briefing

A morning brief that gives the CMO a clear picture of the day ahead: what's on the calendar, what requires a decision, what to know going into each conversation, and anything in the news or business that's relevant. Paste in the schedule and any context you have — Claude structures it into a scannable brief the CMO can read in two minutes.

Example Prompt

"Build a daily briefing for the CMO for [date]. Here is today's schedule: [paste calendar]. Here is any relevant context for each meeting: [paste notes]. Also include: (1) any decisions or approvals needed today, (2) anything from the news or business landscape worth knowing, (3) one priority to flag at the top. Format as a clean, scannable brief — short headers, tight bullets, no filler. The CMO should be able to read this in under two minutes."

Week Ahead Report

A Sunday or Monday morning view of the full week: what's coming, what needs preparation, what decisions are on the horizon, and where the CMO's attention should be focused. Built from the calendar and any business context you have, this gives the CMO a running start on Monday rather than catching up all week.

Example Prompt

"Build a Week Ahead report for the CMO for the week of [date]. Here is the full week calendar: [paste]. Here is relevant business context: [paste any campaign updates, approvals pending, leadership priorities, or upcoming deadlines]. Structure it as: (1) Priority focus for the week — the 2-3 things that matter most, (2) Day-by-day view — key meetings and what to know for each, (3) Decisions needed this week — anything requiring the CMO's sign-off, (4) Watch list — anything developing that may need attention. Keep it tight. This should feel like a smart briefing, not a calendar printout."

Upcoming Engagement Prep

Before any high-stakes meeting, event, or appearance — a one-page prep document that gives the CMO everything they need to walk in confident. Who's in the room, what they care about, what the CMO should accomplish, likely questions, and two or three things to know going in. Works for internal reviews, external meetings, industry events, and media appearances.

Example Prompt

"Build an engagement prep document for the CMO for [meeting/event name] on [date]. Here is the context: [describe the meeting — who is attending, the purpose, what the CMO is expected to do or say, and any background you have on the attendees or topic]. Include: (1) Purpose — what this meeting is trying to accomplish, (2) Who's in the room — key attendees with a one-line note on each, (3) What the CMO should walk out with — the goal, (4) Likely questions or topics to be ready for, (5) Key things to know going in — context, history, or sensitivities. One page. Tight. The CMO should be able to read it in the car."

Meeting Notes to Action Items

After any meeting, turn raw notes into a clean action item summary: who owns what, by when, and what the key decisions were. Paste in rough notes — even messy ones — and Claude extracts the structure. Saves time and makes sure nothing falls through after the CMO's busiest meetings.

Example Prompt

"Here are raw notes from a meeting with [attendees] on [date]: [paste notes]. Extract and organize into: (1) Key decisions made, (2) Action items — each with owner name, task description, and due date if mentioned, (3) Open questions — things that came up but weren't resolved, (4) Any follow-ups the CMO needs to be aware of. Format as a clean summary I can send to attendees. Flag anything where the owner or deadline wasn't clear."

Executive Correspondence Drafting

When the CMO needs to send a message and you're drafting it — a response to a senior leader, a note to a partner, a follow-up from a meeting. Claude drafts in the CMO's voice when you give it enough context. You review and adjust tone. Works for emails, brief notes, and longer letters.

Example Prompt

"Draft an email from [CMO name] to [recipient and their role]. Purpose: [what this email needs to accomplish]. Context: [background — what happened before this email, any relationship context, what tone is appropriate]. Key points to include: [list]. Preferred length: [short and direct / one paragraph / full note]. Write in a voice that is confident, warm, and senior — not overly formal. Give me two versions: one slightly warmer, one more direct."

Stakeholder Background Brief

When the CMO is meeting someone new — or someone they haven't met with in a while — a quick brief on who they are, what they care about, and how to approach the conversation. Works for internal stakeholders, external partners, and industry relationships. Give Claude what you know and ask it to structure it into a useful pre-meeting read.

Example Prompt

"Build a stakeholder brief for the CMO before a meeting with [name and title] on [date]. Here is what I know about them: [paste any background — their role, company, past interactions, public profile, or context]. The meeting is about: [purpose]. Include: (1) Who they are — role, background, what they are known for, (2) What they likely care about in this meeting, (3) How to approach the conversation — tone, priorities, any sensitivities, (4) One thing the CMO should know going in that will make the conversation go better. Keep it to half a page."

Office of the CMO Pro Tip

Set up a Claude Project specifically for the CMO's office. Load it with the CMO's priorities, communication style, current focus areas, and key relationships. Every brief, prep doc, and correspondence draft you run through that Project will already know the context. You stop re-explaining the same background and start getting outputs that are ready to use from the first draft.
